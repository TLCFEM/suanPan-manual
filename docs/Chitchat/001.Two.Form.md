# Two Forms of Storytelling

If you have studied a sufficient number of finite element method textbooks, you have likely noticed that content organization follows at least two distinct patterns.
Viewing textbooks as narratives that convey the principles of FEM, I refer to these as two narrative approaches.

The first approach emphasizes engineering applications, beginning with truss system analysis, developing element stiffness matrices followed by the global assembly, and outlining a general solution strategy --- similar to the matrix stiffness method taught in structural analysis courses.
This provides readers with an initial understanding of FEM's practical applications.
After gaining this foundation, two-dimensional elements are presented, followed by increasingly advanced topics.

The second approach adopts a more mathematical perspective, beginning with partial differential equations and self-adjoint operators, then proceeding to the strong and weak forms of differential equations.
Building upon the weak form, the Galerkin method is introduced, which naturally leads to shape functions, isoparametric mapping, and related concepts.
This approach establishes the rigorous mathematical foundations underlying FEM.
